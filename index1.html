Gun Violence Dashboard

Matthias Olson Professor Cameron Economics of Crime March 12, 2025 Gun violence is a highly debated and pressing issue in the United States, and with increasing rates of firearm deaths, injuries, and mass shootings, a deeper understanding is essential for informed public policy decisions. This project draws on the Gun Violence Archive data from 2014 to 2017 and utilizes Tableau dashboards to visualize population-adjusted firearm injuries and deaths across the U.S. A hex map reveals that southern states like Louisiana and Alabama consistently report higher rates of firearm incidents per capita, whereas northeastern states like Rhode Island and Massachusetts tend to have lower rates. The calendar heatmap visualization further uncovers that weekends and summer months witness heightened gun violence incidents, potentially correlating with social events and outdoor activities. The bump chart ranking states by annual impact highlights shifts in top offenders, illustrating, for instance, that Alaska’s rankings fluctuate while Louisiana remains consistently high. These findings underscore the regional and temporal nature of gun violence, reinforcing the need for both localized and time-sensitive intervention strategies. By translating raw data into accessible visual patterns, this project supports a clearer, data-driven understanding of gun violence trends. The intention is not only to inform readers but to stimulate public discussion and contribute to policy development focused on reducing gun-related harm across the country.

U.S. Crime Trends Dashboard

Matthias Olson Professor Cameron Economics of Crime March 26, 2025 This dashboard explores changes in violent and property crime in the United States using publicly available FBI data. Using Tableau, I created visualizations to identify regional patterns and long-term trends in these crime categories. The report specifically highlights how crime rates have changed across census regions and within the state of South Carolina from 1960 to 2022. The first chart shows violent crime trends by region, clearly indicating that the South has historically experienced higher violent crime rates compared to other regions. Property crime has followed a similar trajectory across regions, though rates have declined significantly since their peak in the early 1990s. One of the most striking insights comes from the South Carolina focus section, where burglary and larceny have both declined over the past three decades. Despite South Carolina's reputation for high crime, the data indicates substantial improvement. Finally, a state-by-state scatter plot provides a snapshot of how each state currently ranks in terms of both violent and property crime. The insights generated here allow policymakers and researchers to make meaningful comparisons and to target reforms to the areas most in need. By organizing complex datasets into accessible visual formats, this project supports a more nuanced understanding of crime dynamics in the U.S. and emphasizes the importance of disaggregating data by time and geography to inform public safety efforts.

Incarceration Trends Dashboard

Matthias Olson Professor Cameron Economics of Crime April 16, 2025 Using county-level data from the Vera Institute of Justice’s Incarceration Trends Dataset, this dashboard examines South Carolina’s jail and prison incarceration rates. The data spans from 1970 to 2018, covering changes in incarceration for rural and urban counties alike. Tableau visualizations help reveal which counties are driving the state’s high incarceration rates and whether trends differ by population density. The choropleth maps show that some rural counties, such as Allendale and McCormick, have exceptionally high incarceration rates, particularly in jails. Conversely, counties like Charleston and Greenville show more moderate incarceration patterns. These spatial differences suggest that local policy and enforcement practices play a major role in incarceration trends. Stacked area charts track the evolution of incarceration over time. While both prison and jail populations peaked in the mid-2000s, they have declined slightly in recent years, aligning with national decarceration efforts. This project reinforces the idea that incarceration in the U.S. is not only shaped by federal and state policy but also by localized legal structures. Effective reform must consider this variation if it hopes to meaningfully reduce incarceration rates. Presenting these insights visually allows for clearer identification of disparities and more targeted recommendations for reform.

Campus Crime Dashboard

Matthias Olson Professor Cameron Economics of Crime April 30, 2025 This dashboard investigates reported crimes at five major universities in South Carolina: USC, Clemson, Coastal Carolina, College of Charleston, and Winthrop University. Using Clery Act data on sexual misconduct, aggravated assault, and burglary, the dashboard allows for comparative analysis using rates per 100,000 students. The line graph of sexual misconduct over time shows inconsistent but alarming spikes in reports. Coastal Carolina, for example, experienced a sudden jump in 2019. Similarly, the treemap ranking universities by average aggravated assault per 100,000 students reveals Coastal Carolina to be a consistent outlier. Burglary data suggests housing-related risks are higher at universities with less secure dormitories or those located in more urban areas. The bar chart makes these differences clear by showing a wide gap between the highest and lowest burglary rates. Taken together, these dashboards demonstrate that campus crime varies significantly across institutions, even within the same state. They also emphasize the importance of context—enrollment size, reporting culture, and campus security policies all play a role. Policymakers and campus administrators can use these insights to identify vulnerable populations and allocate resources more effectively.

Global Terrorism Dashboard

This global terrorism dashboard visualizes regional and temporal trends in terrorist activity (1970–2019), showing variation in attack types and regional prevalence using bubble and stacked area charts from the Global Terrorism Database. The goal is to understand how violence manifests across regions, time, and tactics. Bombings and assassinations dominate in some regions, while hostage-taking and armed assault are more prominent in others. By examining these differences, the dashboard helps highlight both global patterns and regional nuance in terrorist activity.
